Thinking in Measures

Brief notes on papers and core insights gathered throughout my studies: the central idea, why it matters, and where it can be applied. Posts on this page stay readable while keeping mathematical precision. For each \(\alpha \in I\), there exists an adapted mild solution \(\bar u_\alpha\) of

\[ \bar u_\alpha(t) = S(t)\bar u_{\alpha,0} + \int_0^t S(t-s)\left(\int_I\!\int_H g\!\bigl(\bar u_\alpha(s),y\bigr)\,\mu_{\beta,s}(dy)\,\eta^\alpha(d\beta)\right)\,ds \] \[ + \int_0^t S(t-s)\left(\int_I\!\int_H h\!\bigl(\bar u_\alpha(s),y\bigr)\,\mu_{\beta,s}(dy)\,\hat\eta^\alpha(d\beta)\right)\,dW^Q_\alpha(s), \] \[ \mu_{\beta,t}:=(\mathrm{ev}_t)_\#\mu_\beta. \]

15 min read - Gaussian free fields, Gaussian multiplicative chaos, conformal field theory, probability

This research project studies a remarkably explicit random object arising from a highly singular field. The protagonist is the partition function of Gaussian multiplicative chaos on the unit circle. At first glance, the object looks too rough to admit a clean closed formula: it is built from the exponential of a Gaussian free field, and the latter is not even defined pointwise in the classical sense. Yet the central message is that this rough probabilistic quantity hides a rigid conformal structure, and that this structure can be exploited to compute its law exactly.

The route taken here is not merely probabilistic. The project places the problem inside Liouville conformal field theory and uses the BPZ equations as the main analytic engine. In that sense, the project is about far more than one formula: it is about showing how a random measure, a conformal field theory, and a special function computation lock together with surprising precision.

\[ Y_\gamma = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} e^{\frac{\gamma}{2}X(e^{i\theta})}\,d\theta, \qquad \gamma\in(0,2), \]

where \(X\) is the Gaussian free field on the unit circle, understood through a regularization-and-limit procedure. The main theorem identifies the entire moment structure of \(Y_\gamma\). This is the Fyodorov–Bouchaud formula:

\[ \mathbb E\!\left[Y_\gamma^p\right] = \frac{\Gamma\!\left(1-\frac{p\gamma^2}{4}\right)} {\Gamma\!\left(1-\frac{\gamma^2}{4}\right)^p}, \qquad p<\frac{4}{\gamma^2}. \]

Equivalently, the partition function itself has an explicit law in terms of an exponential random variable. So the end product is strikingly simple, but the road to it runs through a rich geometric and analytic landscape. This is exactly the kind of result that makes the subject beautiful: an object born from rough randomness ends up obeying a formula of classical special-function flavor.

The first layer of the project is foundational. It begins with the Gaussian free field, not as an honest function, but as a random distribution. This matters because the covariance kernel behaves logarithmically on the diagonal, which means pointwise values are too singular to make direct sense. One therefore regularizes the field, for instance through circle averages or smooth convolutions, and studies the resulting family \(X_\varepsilon\). The guiding principle is that the field itself is rough, but its regularizations are smooth enough to manipulate.

\[ M_\varepsilon(dz) = e^{\gamma X_\varepsilon(z)-\frac{\gamma^2}{2}\mathbb E[X_\varepsilon(z)^2]}\,\sigma(dz), \]

and the Gaussian multiplicative chaos measure is obtained by sending \(\varepsilon\downarrow 0\). This renormalization is not cosmetic; it is what removes the logarithmic divergence and leaves behind a nontrivial random measure. In loose terms, the project starts by taming a random distribution and turning it into a random geometry.

From there the perspective shifts toward Liouville conformal field theory. This is one of the elegant moves of the project: instead of treating the partition function as an isolated probabilistic integral, it is embedded into a much broader family of correlation functions. These are built from vertex operators, which in the upper half-plane take the form

\[ V_\alpha(z) = e^{\alpha\left(X(z)+\frac{Q}{2}\log \hat g(z)\right)}, \qquad Q=\frac{2}{\gamma}+\frac{\gamma}{2}. \]

Once this field-theoretic language is in place, the problem becomes one of understanding how certain correlation functions vary as the insertion points move. That is where the BPZ equations enter. Roughly speaking, the insertion of a special degenerate field forces the correlation function to satisfy a second-order differential equation. This creates a bridge from randomness to analysis.

One of the conceptual high points of the research project is that it does not stop at the standard BPZ relation. It introduces a tweaked regularization with an additional parameter \(\eta\), and then asks: for which values of the parameters does a BPZ equation still hold? This is one of the genuine novelties emphasized in the manuscript.

\[ \left( \frac{1}{\alpha_1^2}\partial_{z_1z_1} + \frac{\Delta_{\alpha_1}}{(z_1-\bar z_1)^2} + \frac{\Delta_{\alpha_2}}{(z_1-z_2)^2} + \frac{\Delta_{\alpha_2}}{(z_1-\bar z_2)^2} + \frac{1}{z_1-\bar z_1}\partial_{\bar z_1} + \frac{1}{z_1-z_2}\partial_{z_2} + \frac{1}{z_1-\bar z_2}\partial_{\bar z_2} \right) \langle V_{\alpha_1}(z_1)V_{\alpha_2}(z_2)\rangle_{H,\eta} =0. \]

The project shows that this generalized BPZ equation holds precisely for the distinguished parameter choices \((\alpha_1,\eta)=(-\gamma/2,\gamma/2)\) in the primal regime and \((\alpha_1,\eta)=(-2/\gamma,2/\gamma)\) in the dual regime. This is mathematically satisfying because it explains why the usual degenerate insertion is not a miraculous guess: it is singled out by an exact compatibility condition.

The proof strategy is computational in a good sense. It repeatedly uses Girsanov’s theorem to shift the Gaussian field, derives an integration-by-parts identity for correlation functions, computes explicit covariance terms of the regularized field, and then shows that the metric-dependent pieces cancel. That cancellation is one of the elegant structural moments of the argument: the geometry initially appears to complicate the derivatives, but in the end the unwanted terms conspire to vanish.

A second novelty is that the project extends this philosophy beyond the two-point situation and formulates a generalized BPZ equation for \(n\)-point correlation functions. So the work is not only reproducing a known derivation of one scalar law; it is clarifying the larger mechanism behind it and showing how the same machinery scales to a broader class of observables.

Once the BPZ equation is in hand, the next step is geometric. The project passes from the upper half-plane to the unit disk by conformal maps and then uses Möbius transformations to place the marked points in a canonical position. The dependence on two complex variables is thereby compressed into a single real parameter

\[ t = \left|\frac{z_1-z_2}{z_1-\bar z_2}\right|, \qquad t\in(0,1). \]

This is more than a cosmetic simplification. It turns the BPZ partial differential equation into an ordinary differential equation in the variable \(t\), and this ODE is of hypergeometric type. At that moment the entire problem changes flavor: the random partition function is now encoded in the asymptotics of a hypergeometric solution.

The project then introduces a function \(G(\gamma,p,t)\) whose value at \(t=0\) and \(t=1\) controls the negative moments of the partition function. Hypergeometric expansions at the endpoints produce relations between coefficients, and these relations become shift equations for the moments. In other words, the probabilistic quantity \(\mathbb E[Y_\gamma^p]\) is recovered not by direct integration, but by solving a rigid analytic matching problem.

The logic is subtle and elegant. First one controls negative integer moments. Then Fourier-analytic arguments extend the result to all negative moments. Finally, the change of variables \(x\mapsto 1/x\) transfers the information to positive moments, which completes the derivation of the full Fyodorov–Bouchaud formula.

A useful way to summarize the proof is the following chain:

\[ \text{GFF} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{GMC} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{LCFT correlation functions} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{BPZ equation} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{hypergeometric ODE} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{shift relation} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{exact law of }Y_\gamma. \]

The research project does not end with the theorem itself. Another substantial contribution is that it studies consequences and viewpoints that help place the formula in a wider mathematical picture. One direction is the semiclassical limit, where one examines what happens as the coupling becomes small. In that regime the random geometry begins to resemble a more classical object, and the project discusses both uniform integrability and large-deviation behavior. This matters because exact formulas are strongest when one can also read asymptotic structure from them.

A further direction is the study of rare events. Here the formula is not just a moment identity: it becomes a window into the tail behavior of the chaos partition function. That is conceptually important because Gaussian multiplicative chaos is precisely about exceptional concentration of mass, and rare-event analysis tells us how costly that concentration is.

Then comes the dynamical viewpoint. The project connects BPZ equations with Schramm–Loewner evolutions. In this interpretation, certain three-point correlation functions become actual probabilities for SLE paths, for instance the probability that a curve passes to the left of a marked point. This is a beautiful dictionary entry: a conformal field theoretic correlation function becomes a stochastic-geometric event.

Finally, the algebraic viewpoint explains why BPZ equations should exist at all. The project discusses the Witt algebra, its central extension into the Virasoro algebra, highest-weight states, and the role of degenerate representations. From that perspective, differential equations are not accidental outputs of a computation; they are shadows of algebraic constraints in the representation theory. This gives the whole story a second life: one may read the project analytically, probabilistically, geometrically, or algebraically, and the same formula keeps reappearing.

The deepest novelty introduced in this research project is therefore not a single line of computation, but a sharpening of the structural picture. The project presents the classical Fyodorov–Bouchaud formula through LCFT, identifies and proves a generalized BPZ equation with a tweaked regularization parameter, extends that framework to \(n\)-point functions, and then develops consequences ranging from semiclassical asymptotics to dynamical and algebraic interpretations. It turns an exact law into a crossroads of several theories.

If one wanted to keep only one idea from this story, it would be this: the exponential of a rough Gaussian field appears at first as an unruly random object, but once it is placed inside the correct conformal framework, it becomes rigid enough to solve exactly. The randomness is genuine, the singularities are genuine, yet the final answer is governed by symmetry. That tension between roughness and rigidity is the real heart of the project.

\[ Y_\gamma \stackrel{d}{=} \frac{1}{\Gamma\!\left(1-\frac{\gamma^2}{4}\right)\,E(1)^{\gamma^2/4}}, \]

Impact: this research project shows how exact probability laws for Gaussian multiplicative chaos can be extracted from conformal symmetry, and how one formula can illuminate hypergeometric analysis, rare events, SLE, and Virasoro algebra all at once.
