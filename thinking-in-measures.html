Thesis spoiler: what my research is about

My master’s thesis studies mean-field limits for stochastic interacting particle systems when the state space is no longer finite-dimensional, but a separable Hilbert space. The guiding question is how to extend the digraph measure (DGM) framework to settings where each particle is itself an evolving function, field, or profile, so that the limit is described on path space rather than just on \(\mathbb{R}^d\).

The central object is a label-dependent mild equation: for each graph label \(\alpha \in I\), the limiting process evolves under a semigroup, while its drift and noise are averaged against a family of laws indexed by the surrounding network structure. In other words, the thesis connects graph limits, Wasserstein geometry on path space, and stochastic evolution equations to describe how large random networks of infinite-dimensional particles behave in the limit.

\[ \begin{aligned} \bar u_\alpha(t) &= S(t)\bar u_{\alpha,0} + \int_0^t S(t-s) \left( \int_I \!\int_H g\!\bigl(\bar u_\alpha(s),y\bigr)\, \mu_{\beta,s}(dy)\,\eta^\alpha(d\beta) \right) ds \\ &\quad + \int_0^t S(t-s) \left( \int_I \!\int_H h\!\bigl(\bar u_\alpha(s),y\bigr)\, \mu_{\beta,s}(dy)\,\hat\eta^\alpha(d\beta) \right) dW_\alpha^Q(s), \\ \mu_{\beta,t}:&=(\mathrm{ev}_t)_\#\mu_\beta . \end{aligned} \]

FB Formula and BPZ Equations

This research project studies an object which at first seems too singular to admit an exact law: the partition function of Gaussian multiplicative chaos on the unit circle. The surprise is that, once the problem is placed in the language of Liouville conformal field theory, the randomness becomes rigid enough to satisfy differential equations, hypergeometric identities, and eventually an explicit distributional formula.

The project is not only about proving one famous identity. It also clarifies why the relevant BPZ equation appears, proposes a generalized BPZ framework, studies semiclassical and rare-event consequences, and then reinterprets the same structure dynamically through SLEs and algebraically through the Virasoro algebra. In that sense, the formula is the center of the story, but not the whole story.

Foundations

The starting point is the Gaussian free field, not as an honest function, but as a random distribution with covariance given by a Green kernel. This already explains why the problem is delicate: in two dimensions the covariance has logarithmic singularities, so one cannot simply evaluate the field pointwise and exponentiate it.

\[ \Delta G(x,\cdot)=\delta_x(\cdot), \]

together with appropriate boundary conditions, is the equation underlying the covariance kernel. The field is then interpreted distributionally, and one regularizes it by smooth approximations \(X_\varepsilon\). This lets one define the Gaussian multiplicative chaos through a renormalized exponential.

\[ M_\varepsilon(dz) = e^{\gamma X_\varepsilon(z)-\frac{\gamma^2}{2}\mathbb{E}[X_\varepsilon(z)^2]}\,\sigma(dz), \qquad \gamma\in(0,2). \]

The limit of these measures, as \(\varepsilon\downarrow 0\), is the GMC measure. This is the random geometry behind the whole project. The logarithmic divergence is not removed by magic, but by the exact renormalization above. What remains is a nontrivial random measure with a very rich moment structure.

We then shift toward Liouville conformal field theory. This is an essential move: instead of studying a single random integral in isolation, the project embeds it into a family of correlation functions built from vertex operators.

\[ V_\alpha(z) = e^{\alpha\left(X(z)+\frac{Q}{2}\log \hat g(z)\right)}, \qquad \Delta_\alpha=\frac{\alpha}{2}\left(Q-\frac{\alpha}{2}\right), \qquad Q=\frac{2}{\gamma}+\frac{\gamma}{2}. \]

So, this first part builds the dictionary: rough field \(\to\) regularization \(\to\) chaos measure \(\to\) Liouville correlation functions. Without that dictionary, the main formula would look isolated. With it, the formula becomes one manifestation of a larger conformal structure.

Fyodorov–Bouchaud Formula: Statement and Proof

The central random variable of the project is the partition function

\[ Y_\gamma = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} e^{\frac{\gamma}{2}X(e^{i\theta})}\,d\theta, \qquad \gamma\in(0,2). \]

The main theorem states that for every \(p<4/\gamma^2\),

\[ \mathbb{E}[Y_\gamma^p] = \frac{\Gamma\!\left(1-\frac{p\gamma^2}{4}\right)} {\Gamma\!\left(1-\frac{\gamma^2}{4}\right)^p}. \]

Equivalently,

\[ Y_\gamma \stackrel{d}{=} \frac{1}{\Gamma\!\left(1-\frac{\gamma^2}{4}\right)\,E(1)^{\gamma^2/4}}, \]

where \(E(1)\) is an exponential random variable of parameter \(1\). This is the Fyodorov–Bouchaud formula. The final expression is classical in appearance, but the object it describes is genuinely singular.

The project proves this result through LCFT, and here one of its main novelties appears: instead of only using the classical degenerate insertion, it introduces a tweaked regularization with an extra parameter \(\eta\) and asks for which values a BPZ equation still survives. This makes precise why the usual choice is special.

\[ \left( \frac{1}{\alpha_1^2}\partial_{z_1z_1} + \frac{\Delta_{\alpha_1}}{(z_1-\bar z_1)^2} + \frac{\Delta_{\alpha_2}}{(z_1-z_2)^2} + \frac{\Delta_{\alpha_2}}{(z_1-\bar z_2)^2} + \frac{1}{z_1-\bar z_1}\partial_{\bar z_1} + \frac{1}{z_1-z_2}\partial_{z_2} + \frac{1}{z_1-\bar z_2}\partial_{\bar z_2} \right) \langle V_{\alpha_1}(z_1)V_{\alpha_2}(z_2)\rangle_{H,\eta} =0. \]

The project shows that this holds precisely for the distinguished choices \[ (\alpha_1,\eta)=\left(-\frac{\gamma}{2},\frac{\gamma}{2}\right) \quad\text{or}\quad (\alpha_1,\eta)=\left(-\frac{2}{\gamma},\frac{2}{\gamma}\right). \] So the usual degenerate insertion is not merely convenient: it is singled out by an exact compatibility condition.

The proof mechanism is elegant and concrete. Girsanov’s theorem is used repeatedly to shift the Gaussian field, derive integration-by-parts identities, and compute derivatives of the correlation function. Metric-dependent terms initially appear, but they cancel out thanks to a structural identity. This is one of the most important parts of the project: geometry seems to complicate the formulas, and then the geometry itself removes the complication.

After that, the problem is pushed from the upper half-plane to the unit disk by conformal maps. A Möbius transform then compresses the geometry of two marked points into a single parameter

\[ t = \left|\frac{z_1-z_2}{z_1-\bar z_2}\right|, \qquad t\in(0,1). \]

This is the geometric step that turns the BPZ PDE into a hypergeometric ODE. Once that happens, the negative moments of the partition function can be encoded in a hypergeometric expansion. The asymptotics at \(t=0\) and \(t=1\) produce shift relations, and those relations ultimately reconstruct the entire moment formula.

Another novelty is that the generalized BPZ mechanism is not restricted to two-point functions. The project extends it to \(n\)-point correlation functions. This part then clarifies the structural reason behind the proof and pushes it into a broader framework.

Fyodorov–Bouchaud Formula: Consequences

Once the exact law is known, the project turns to asymptotic consequences. The first one is the semiclassical limit, where the coupling becomes small and the random object starts to exhibit a more classical behavior. This is where exact formulas become especially powerful: they can be expanded, rescaled, and interpreted.

The chapter also studies rare events through large deviations. This is a natural question in Gaussian multiplicative chaos, since the measure is designed precisely to capture highly uneven concentrations of mass. The rate function then describes how unlikely certain rescaled values become as the coupling tends to zero.

In words, this part asks: once the partition function is explicit, what does it tell us about typical behavior, atypical behavior, and the transition between the two? That is why this chapter is more than an appendix to the theorem. It shows that the exact law has real asymptotic content.

BPZ Equations: A Dynamical and Algebraic Viewpoint

The last chapter of the project gives it its broadest perspective. The same BPZ equations that controlled the GMC partition function are now re-read dynamically through Schramm–Loewner evolutions and algebraically through the Virasoro algebra. This is one of the most appealing aspects of the project: the same differential equation belongs at once to probability, complex dynamics, and representation theory.

4.1 BPZ Equations and SLEs

The dynamical viewpoint begins with the chordal Loewner equation on the upper half-plane:

\[ \begin{cases} \displaystyle \frac{\partial}{\partial t}g_t(z)=\frac{2}{g_t(z)-\sqrt{\kappa}\,B_t},\\[1ex] g_0(z)=z. \end{cases} \]

This is the SLE flow. One should think of \(g_t\) as a random conformal evolution driven by Brownian motion. The project then broadens the picture by allowing a semimartingale driver

\[ dX_t=\kappa\,b(X_t)\,dt+\sqrt{\kappa}\,dB_t, \qquad X_0=0. \]

Via Girsanov, this can be related back to the Brownian-driven case, so the generalized setting is not disconnected from the classical one. It is a controlled deformation of it.

A key idea in the chapter is that the conformal images of marked points, together with the derivatives of the Loewner map, naturally generate martingales whenever a certain differential equation holds. Differentiating the Loewner equation in \(z\) gives

\[ \partial_t g_t'(z) = -\frac{2\,g_t'(z)}{(g_t(z)-X_t)^2}, \qquad dg_t'(z) = -\frac{2\,g_t'(z)}{(g_t(z)-X_t)^2}\,dt. \]

From this the project introduces

\[ G_t:=\prod_{i=1}^n g_t'(z_i)^{\Delta_{\alpha_i}}, \]

and derives the evolution

\[ dG_t = -2G_t\sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\Delta_{\alpha_i}}{(g_t(z_i)-X_t)^2}\,dt. \]

This is the point where the BPZ structure enters dynamically. If one writes

\[ M_t(z_1,\dots,z_n) = G_t\,h(g_t(z_1),\dots,g_t(z_n)), \]

then the project shows that \(M_t\) is a local martingale if and only if \(h\) satisfies a BPZ-type PDE. In the SLE setting recorded in the project, the condition is

\[ \left( \frac{\kappa}{2} \sum_{i,j=1}^n \partial_x\Phi(t,x,g_t^{-1}(y_i)) \partial_x\Phi(t,x,g_t^{-1}(y_j)) \frac{\partial^2}{\partial y_i\partial y_j} + \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{2}{y_i-x}\frac{\partial}{\partial y_i} - \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{2\Delta_{\alpha_i}}{(y_i-x)^2} \right) h(y_1,\dots,y_n)=0. \]

So the BPZ equation is no longer just a static conformal-field-theoretic identity. It becomes exactly the condition that makes a naturally constructed SLE observable into a martingale. This is a very clean dynamical meaning.

The project goes further and records the stochastic differential of the martingale itself:

\[ dM_t = \sqrt{\kappa}\,G_t \sum_{i=1}^n \partial_x\Phi(t,X_t,z_i)\, (\partial_i h)(g_t(z_1),\dots,g_t(z_n))\,dB_t. \]

When this local martingale is a true martingale, one gets a mean-value representation:

\[ h(z_1,\dots,z_n) = \mathbb{E}\!\left[ h(g_t(z_1),\dots,g_t(z_n)) \prod_{i=1}^n \bigl((\partial_z\Phi)(t,X_t,z_i)\bigr)^{\Delta_{\alpha_i}} \right]. \]

This is a particularly nice formula. It says that the correlation-type quantity \(h\) can be recovered as an average of its image under the random conformal flow, weighted by the conformal derivatives. In other words, BPZ observables are transported by the SLE dynamics in a martingale-preserving way.

The chapter then reaches one of its most beautiful interpretations: certain three-point correlation functions are actual probabilities. More precisely, if one considers an SLE from \(0\) to \(\infty\), the probability that the curve passes to the left of a marked point \(z_1\in\mathbb H\) is identified with a Liouville three-point function:

\[ \mathbb{P}\!\left(\text{SLE from }0\text{ to }\infty\text{ passes to the left of }z_1\right) = \bigl\langle V_{-\gamma/2}(z_1)V_{\alpha_2}(0)V_{\alpha_3}(\infty)\bigr\rangle_{H,\gamma/2}. \]

This is the point where the project’s dynamical viewpoint becomes especially vivid. A correlation function is no longer merely a formal object in a field theory; it is the probability of a geometric event involving a random curve. That is a remarkable bridge between LCFT and stochastic planar geometry.

The project even suggests a possible discrete analogue: a meromorphic random dynamical system with noisy iteration, meant as a discrete counterpart to the continuous Loewner flow. This gives the chapter a forward-looking quality. It does not only reinterpret known equations; it hints at further directions in random complex dynamics.

4.2 BPZ Equations and Virasoro Algebras

The final section explains why BPZ equations should exist from an algebraic point of view. The Witt algebra is generated by vector fields

\[ L_n := z^{1-n}\frac{d}{dz}, \qquad [L_n,L_m]=(n-m)L_{n+m}, \qquad n,m\in\mathbb Z. \]

Its central extension is the Virasoro algebra. In the background of BPZ theory lies the fact that, for special conformal weights, the corresponding representations become degenerate. Those degeneracies force the conformal blocks to satisfy linear differential equations. The BPZ equations are thus not accidental analytic facts; they are shadows of representation-theoretic constraints.

This closes the circle of the project. A random partition function from Gaussian multiplicative chaos leads to LCFT correlation functions; those satisfy BPZ equations; the same BPZ equations appear as martingale conditions in SLE; and their existence is explained algebraically by Virasoro degeneracy. The formula sits at the meeting point of all these viewpoints.

Impact: this research project shows that the partition function of Gaussian multiplicative chaos on the circle is not merely computable, but structurally rigid. Its exact law is uncovered through LCFT, its special differential equations are clarified through a generalized BPZ framework, its asymptotics reveal semiclassical and rare-event behavior, and its dynamical meaning emerges through SLE martingales and geometric probabilities.

BF-Calculus and Spectral Theorem

This bachelor thesis studies how one can pass from the familiar finite-dimensional spectral theorem to a fully infinite-dimensional representation theorem for bounded self-adjoint operators on separable Hilbert spaces. The central problem is not merely to diagonalize an operator in an abstract sense, but to construct a precise rule that assigns an operator f(M) to every bounded Borel-measurable function f on the spectrum of M.

The final target is a spectral representation in which the operator becomes multiplication by the scalar function itself. In this sense, the whole thesis may be read as a carefully organized passage from algebraic expressions in powers of M to measurable functional expressions over its spectrum. The main point is that self-adjoint operators are not only objects that admit eigenvalue-like decompositions in finite dimensions, but also operators that can be understood through measure-theoretic and topological machinery in the infinite-dimensional setting.

\[ f(M)=\int_{\sigma(M)} f\,dP=\mathcal{U}\mathcal{M}_f\mathcal{U}^* \]

Here, \(\mathcal{M}_f\) denotes multiplication by \(f\) on a suitable Hilbert space of sections, \(P\) is the spectral measure associated with \(M\), and \(\mathcal{U}\) is an isometric isomorphism transporting multiplication on the spectral side back to the original Hilbert space.

\[ \begin{array}{ccc} L^2(\sigma(M);\chi) & \xrightarrow{\ \mathcal{M}_f\ } & L^2(\sigma(M);\chi) \\ \mathcal{U}\downarrow & & \downarrow\mathcal{U} \\ H & \xrightarrow{\ f(M)\ } & H \end{array} \]

Chapter 1 — Finite-Dimensional Spectral Theorem.

The thesis begins in the finite-dimensional setting, where the spectral theorem is already known from linear algebra. If \(M=M^*\in\mathcal L(H)\) and \(H\cong \ell^2(\{1,\dots,n\})\), then there exists a unitary operator \(U\) and a diagonal operator \(\Lambda=\mathrm{diag}(\lambda_1,\dots,\lambda_n)\) such that \[ M=U\Lambda U^*. \] This is the point where the geometric structure first becomes visible: the operator is decomposed into spectral blocks, and each eigenvalue \(\lambda\) comes with an orthogonal projection onto its eigenspace.

Instead of viewing this merely as diagonalization, the thesis rewrites the operator as a sum over spectral projections:

\[ M=\sum_{\lambda\in\sigma(M)} \lambda P_\lambda. \]

This already suggests a functional calculus. Indeed, if one can define \(f(M)\) by acting on each spectral block with the scalar \(f(\lambda)\), then one is naturally led to

\[ f(M)=\sum_{\lambda\in\sigma(M)} f(\lambda)P_\lambda. \]

The crucial insight is that the spectral theorem is not only a statement about one operator \(M\), but also about an entire algebra of operators generated from \(M\) by functions. This finite-dimensional model serves as the conceptual prototype for the rest of the thesis: later, sums will become integrals, eigenspaces will become measurable fibers, and diagonal multiplication will survive as the correct final form.

Chapter 2 — Overture.

The second chapter prepares the technical framework. At first sight, it may seem far removed from spectral theory: it introduces initial topologies, weak and weak* topologies, nets, dense extension principles, and measurable Hilbert bundles. Yet this chapter is essential because the later extension procedures are not purely algebraic; they depend on choosing the right topologies and the right notion of continuity.

One of the conceptual pillars is the characterization of convergence in an initial topology. If \(\mathcal F\subseteq Y^X\) is a family of functions and \(X\) is endowed with the weakest topology making all \(f\in\mathcal F\) continuous, then a net \((x_a)\) converges to \(x\) precisely when all observable coordinates converge:

\[ x_a\to x \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \forall f\in\mathcal F:\ f(x_a)\to f(x). \]

This perspective becomes decisive later, when Borel functions and operators are equipped with topologies weaker than the norm topology. Another major ingredient is the construction of measurable Hilbert bundles \(\chi\), together with the associated spaces \(L^2(\Omega;\chi)\). These are the natural infinite-dimensional replacements for the direct sum of eigenspaces from Chapter 1. In finite dimensions one decomposes the Hilbert space into spectral pieces; here one allows the fiber over each point of the spectrum to be a Hilbert space, and one studies square-integrable sections over the whole spectral set.

Thus, Chapter 2 does not yet prove the spectral theorem, but it builds the language in which the final theorem can even be formulated.

Chapter 3 — Continuous Functional Calculus.

The third chapter carries out the first substantial extension step. It starts from polynomials. Given \(p(x)=\sum_{n=0}^N a_nx^n\), one defines

\[ p(M):=\sum_{n=0}^N a_n M^n. \]

This is the most natural possible definition, but the important point is not its formal appearance; it is the structural statement that follows. The polynomial map \(p\mapsto p(M)\) is shown to be a unital, isometric algebra \(*\)-homomorphism. In particular, multiplication, adjoints, and norms are preserved in exactly the way one hopes for. This means the operator calculus respects the algebraic and analytic structure of the function space.

The chapter then proves the spectrum-mapping principle in the polynomial case and extends the entire construction to continuous functions on the compact spectrum \(\sigma(M)\). The key mechanism is Stone–Weierstrass approximation: continuous functions can be approximated uniformly by polynomials, and the operator-side construction is rigid enough to pass to the limit.

\[ \sigma\bigl(f(M)\bigr)=f\bigl(\sigma(M)\bigr), \qquad f\in \mathcal C(\sigma(M)). \]

This chapter is where the thesis first makes clear that the operator \(M\) is being converted into a genuine functional object: one is no longer restricted to algebraic expressions in \(M\), but may apply arbitrary continuous functions to it.

Chapter 4 — Advanced Tools from Topology and Functional Analysis.

The fourth chapter supplies the heavy functional-analytic machinery needed for the next extension step. The transition from continuous functions to bounded Borel-measurable functions is subtler than the transition from polynomials to continuous functions. Uniform approximation is no longer sufficient, and one needs duality, weak* compactness, and operator topologies.

For this reason, the thesis develops Goldstine's theorem, studies weaker notions of convergence, and proves that \(\mathcal L(H)\) is a dual space via trace class operators. Conceptually, the point is that bounded operators must be placed in a setting where weak* arguments become available. Once \(\mathcal L(H)\) is recognized as a dual object, one can exploit bidual extensions without losing control of the algebraic structure established earlier.

This chapter therefore acts as the analytical bridge between the continuous world and the measurable world. It is not ornamental scaffolding: without it, the Borel extension diagram of the next chapter would have no foundation.

Chapter 5 — Borel Functional Calculus.

This is the conceptual center of the thesis. The task is now to extend the continuous functional calculus to all bounded Borel-measurable functions on the spectrum. The guiding idea is that one should not define the extension ad hoc, but rather construct it through a commutative diagram involving biduals and canonical embeddings.

\[ \begin{array}{ccccc} \mathbb K[x]\!\mid_{\sigma(M)} & \subset & \mathcal C(\sigma(M)) & \subset & \mathcal B(\sigma(M)) \\ & & \downarrow \Gamma & & \downarrow \widehat{\Gamma} \\ & & \mathcal L(H) & \subset & \mathcal L(H)'' \end{array} \]

The philosophy is simple but powerful: first understand the continuous calculus as an operator between Banach spaces, then pass to the bidual, and finally return to \(\mathcal L(H)\) through the canonical projection. In this way, one obtains an extension \(f\mapsto f(M)\) for every bounded Borel-measurable \(f\), while preserving the decisive properties of the earlier stages.

The chapter then turns to indicator functions. These are crucial because they generate measurable structure. Once one sets

\[ P(S):=\mathbbm 1_S(M), \]

the operator calculus immediately begins to behave like a measure. Indeed, the family \(P\) becomes a spectral measure, and this is the moment when the thesis shifts from function calculus to operator-valued integration. In finite dimensions, projections onto eigenspaces were the atoms of the theory. Here, indicator functions produce the measurable analogue of those atoms.

Chapter 6 — Integration with Spectral Measures.

Once the spectral measure is available, the next natural step is to integrate with respect to it. The sixth chapter constructs the spectral integral by following the same hierarchy that underlies the ordinary Lebesgue integral: first indicator functions, then simple functions, and finally bounded Borel-measurable functions.

The starting prescription is

\[ \int_\Omega \mathbbm 1_S\,dP := \mathbbm 1_S(M). \]

From there, linearity extends the definition to simple functions, and a uniform approximation argument extends it further to bounded measurable functions. What matters is that the resulting map remains a unital algebra \(*\)-homomorphism and agrees with the Borel functional calculus already constructed in Chapter 5.

\[ \int_\Omega f\,dP \quad\text{is defined so that}\quad f(M)=\int_{\sigma(M)} f\,dP. \]

This identity is more than a notational convenience. It says that the abstract extension procedure of Chapter 5 and the concrete integration procedure of Chapter 6 produce the same operator. Thus the two viewpoints—functional calculus and spectral integration—are proven to be one and the same theory.

Chapter 7 — Bounded Spectral Theorem.

The final chapter gathers every thread of the thesis into a single representation theorem. Starting from a bounded self-adjoint operator \(M\), one first has a Borel functional calculus, then a spectral measure, then a spectral integral. The last step is to show that the whole theory can be represented on a Hilbert space of sections where \(M\) becomes multiplication by the identity function and \(f(M)\) becomes multiplication by \(f\).

The final theorem produces three objects: a probability measure \(\mu\) on the spectrum, a measurable Hilbert bundle \(\chi\) over \(\sigma(M)\), and an isometric isomorphism \[ \mathcal U:L^2(\sigma(M);\chi)\to H. \] With these in hand, one obtains the commuting picture announced at the start:

\[ \begin{array}{ccc} L^2(\sigma(M);\chi) & \xrightarrow{\ \mathcal M_f\ } & L^2(\sigma(M);\chi) \\ \mathcal U\downarrow & & \downarrow\mathcal U \\ H & \xrightarrow{\ f(M)\ } & H \end{array} \]

In particular, taking \(f=\mathrm{id}\) yields the spectral resolution of the operator itself:

\[ M=\mathcal U\mathcal M_{\mathrm{id}}\mathcal U^*. \]

At this stage the analogy with finite-dimensional diagonalization is complete, but the thesis makes clear that the infinite-dimensional result is not obtained by analogy alone. It is built through a chain of carefully justified extensions: polynomial, continuous, Borel, measure-valued, integral, and finally representation-theoretic.

Logical architecture of the thesis.

\[ \text{Finite-dimensional model} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{Polynomial FC} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{Continuous FC} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{Borel FC} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{Spectral Measure} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{Spectral Integral} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{Bounded Spectral Theorem.} \]

This is what gives the thesis its coherence. Each chapter contributes a necessary layer, and the final theorem does not arrive as a sudden formal statement but as the inevitable end point of the preceding structure. The reader is led from a concrete linear-algebraic intuition to an operator-theoretic theorem whose natural home is measure theory and Hilbert space geometry.

Impact: the thesis gives a conceptually transparent route from diagonalization in finite dimensions to the full bounded spectral theorem, showing how topology, duality, measurable structure, and operator algebras cooperate to turn a self-adjoint operator into multiplication on a spectral space.
