Thinking in Measures

Brief notes on papers and core insights gathered throughout my studies: the central idea, why it matters, and where it can be applied. Posts on this page stay readable while keeping mathematical precision. For each \(\alpha \in I\), there exists an adapted mild solution \(\bar u_\alpha\) of

\[ \bar u_\alpha(t) = S(t)\bar u_{\alpha,0} + \int_0^t S(t-s)\left(\int_I\!\int_H g\!\bigl(\bar u_\alpha(s),y\bigr)\,\mu_{\beta,s}(dy)\,\eta^\alpha(d\beta)\right)\,ds \\ + \int_0^t S(t-s)\left(\int_I\!\int_H h\!\bigl(\bar u_\alpha(s),y\bigr)\,\mu_{\beta,s}(dy)\,\hat\eta^\alpha(d\beta)\right)\,dW^Q_\alpha(s), \] \[ \mu_{\beta,t}:=(\mathrm{ev}_t)_\#\mu_\beta. \]

15 min read - probability, Gaussian free fields, multiplicative chaos, conformal field theory, special functions

This note explains the mathematical story behind the paper Fyodorov–Bouchaud Formula and Beyond. At its center is a random quantity built from the Gaussian free field on the unit circle: a boundary partition function whose moments turn out to admit a completely explicit formula. The striking point is not just that the answer is closed-form, but that the route to it goes through several worlds at once: distribution-valued Gaussian fields, Gaussian multiplicative chaos, Liouville conformal field theory, BPZ differential equations, and hypergeometric functions.

The paper is organized as a gradual ascent. First, it builds the Gaussian free field carefully, because the field is too singular to exist pointwise. Then it explains why exponentiating such an object should still make sense after renormalization, which leads to Gaussian multiplicative chaos. Next it moves into Liouville conformal field theory, where carefully chosen vertex operators encode the random partition function of interest. From there, the heart of the proof is to derive a BPZ differential equation, rewrite it as a hypergeometric equation, extract a shift relation for the moments, and finally identify the full law. After that, the paper asks a deeper question: why did the magic work so perfectly here, and why does it fail when one perturbs the setup?

1. The final object and the main theorem

The random variable studied in the paper is

\[ Y_\gamma = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} e^{\frac{\gamma}{2}X(e^{i\theta})}\,d\theta, \qquad \gamma\in(0,2), \]

where \(X\) is the Gaussian free field on the unit circle, understood through a regularization procedure rather than pointwise evaluation. The main result is the Fyodorov–Bouchaud formula:

\[ \mathbb{E}\!\left[Y_\gamma^p\right] = \frac{\Gamma\!\left(1-\frac{p\gamma^2}{4}\right)} {\Gamma\!\left(1-\frac{\gamma^2}{4}\right)^p}, \qquad p<\frac{4}{\gamma^2}. \]

So every admissible moment is explicit. Even better, the paper states the equivalent distributional identity

\[ Y_\gamma \overset{d}{=} \frac{1}{\Gamma\!\left(1-\frac{\gamma^2}{4}\right)\,E(1)^{-\gamma^2/4}}, \]

where \(E(1)\) is an exponential random variable of parameter \(1\). In plain terms, the partition function is not just moment-computable: its law can be identified explicitly from those moments.

2. Why the Gaussian free field is subtle

The Gaussian free field is not an ordinary random function. The paper begins from the Green function \(G(x,y)\) of a boundary value problem and tries to use it as a covariance kernel. Formally one would like to say “take a centered Gaussian field with covariance \(G\).” The problem is that in two dimensions the diagonal blows up logarithmically, so \(G(x,x)=\infty\). That means the field cannot be evaluated pointwise in the usual way.

The workaround is to index the field not by points but by test objects with finite Green energy. If \(\rho_1,\rho_2\) are such objects, one defines

\[ \Gamma_G(\rho_1,\rho_2) := \iint G(x,y)\,d\rho_1(x)\,d\rho_2(y), \]

and then constructs a centered Gaussian process \((X_\rho)_\rho\) with that covariance. So the field is really a random distribution. This matters a lot later, because every exponential involving \(X\) must be interpreted only after smoothing.

The paper then specializes to the unit disk and the upper half-plane with Neumann boundary conditions and a mean-zero constraint. On the disk, the Green function is

\[ G_{\mathbb D}(x,y)=\log \frac{1}{|x-y|\,|1-x\overline y|}, \]

and by conformal transport one gets the corresponding Green function on the upper half-plane. This move is not decorative: the half-plane is the convenient arena for the Liouville CFT calculations, while the disk is the natural place for the boundary partition function.

3. From the GFF to Gaussian multiplicative chaos

Once the field is distribution-valued, expressions like \(e^{\gamma X(z)}\) are meaningless at face value. The paper therefore introduces a regularization \(X_\varepsilon\), for instance via circle averages on the disk or mollification on the half-plane, and defines the renormalized random measure

\[ M_\varepsilon(dz) := e^{\gamma X_\varepsilon(z)-\frac{\gamma^2}{2}\mathbb E[X_\varepsilon(z)^2]}\,\sigma(dz). \]

The subtraction of \(\frac{\gamma^2}{2}\mathbb E[X_\varepsilon(z)^2]\) is the crucial renormalization. Without it, the exponential would blow up as \(\varepsilon\to0\). With it, the paper recalls that for \(\gamma\in(0,2)\) one gets a non-degenerate limit random measure \(M\), independent of the specific regularization scheme. This is Gaussian multiplicative chaos.

Conceptually, GMC is the right way to exponentiate a log-correlated Gaussian field. It is also the probabilistic backbone of Liouville quantum gravity. In the paper, it supplies the random measure hidden inside the Liouville correlation functions and ultimately inside the moments of \(Y_\gamma\).

4. Why Liouville conformal field theory enters

Up to this point the discussion is probabilistic. The next leap is into conformal field theory. The paper introduces Liouville CFT on the upper half-plane with reference metric \(\hat g\), background charge

\[ Q=\frac{2}{\gamma}+\frac{\gamma}{2}, \]

and Polyakov action

\[ S(X,\hat g) = \frac{1}{4\pi}\int_D\!\Big(|\partial_{\hat g}X|^2+Q R_{\hat g}X+4\pi\mu e^{\gamma X}\Big)\,\lambda_{\hat g} + \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{\partial D}\!\Big(QK_{\hat g}X+2\pi\mu_\partial e^{\frac{\gamma}{2}X}\Big)\,\lambda_{\partial\hat g}. \]

The important observables are the vertex operators. In the bulk,

\[ V_\alpha(z)=e^{\alpha\left(X(z)+\frac{Q}{2}\log \hat g(z)\right)}, \]

and on the boundary,

\[ V_\beta(s)=e^{\frac{\beta}{2}\left(X(s)+\frac{Q}{2}\log \hat g(s)\right)}. \]

These are again formal until regularized, but once that is done the corresponding Liouville correlations exist under the Seiberg bounds. The crucial strategic insight of the paper is that the negative moments of the partition function can be recast as special Liouville correlation functions on the disk. So instead of attacking \(\mathbb E[Y_\gamma^p]\) directly, one studies the correct CFT observable.

5. The key mechanism: a BPZ equation for a two-point function

The core technical step is the derivation of a BPZ differential equation for the half-plane correlation with one degenerate insertion:

\[ \big\langle V_{-\gamma/2}(z)\,V_\alpha(z_1)\big\rangle_{\mathbb H}. \]

The paper writes down a second-order PDE for this correlation. The exact expression is a bit long, but its structure is what matters: a second derivative in the degenerate variable \(z\), first derivatives in all marked points, and rational coefficients dictated by conformal weights. Schematically,

\[ \left( \frac{4}{\gamma^2}\partial_{zz} +\text{rational coefficient terms} +\text{first-order derivative terms} \right) \big\langle V_{-\gamma/2}(z)V_\alpha(z_1)\big\rangle_{\mathbb H} =0. \]

This is the moment where probability and CFT genuinely fuse. The proof is not abstract; it is computational, but in a very structured way. The paper uses Gaussian integration by parts obtained from Girsanov’s theorem, computes covariance identities of the regularized GFF, differentiates the regularized correlation, and then shows that all metric-dependent terms cancel through a special identity. That cancellation is one of the hidden miracles of the argument.

One useful way to read this part is as follows. The degenerate insertion \(V_{-\gamma/2}\) is special enough to force a second-order differential equation. The Gaussian structure is strong enough to make the derivatives of the correlation computable. And the exact balance of coefficients is rigid enough that the unwanted pieces disappear. When all three line up, a BPZ equation appears.

6. From BPZ to hypergeometric functions

Once the two-point function is transferred to the disk and rewritten in terms of a single real variable \(t\), the paper obtains an equation for a function \(G(\gamma,p,t)\). After the change of variables \(x=t^2\), this becomes the standard hypergeometric equation

\[ \left( x(1-x)\frac{d^2}{dx^2} +\big(C-(A+B+1)x\big)\frac{d}{dx} -AB \right)H(x)=0, \]

with parameters

\[ A=-\frac{\gamma^2 p}{4},\qquad B=-\frac{\gamma^2}{4},\qquad C=\frac{\gamma^2}{4}(1-p)+1. \]

This is a decisive simplification. Hypergeometric equations have a two-dimensional solution space with well-understood local bases near \(x=0\) and \(x=1\). So the probabilistic problem is reduced to matching the correct asymptotic expansions of a special function.

In other words, the probabilistic complexity gets compressed into two analytic questions: what are the right expansion coefficients, and how are the two local bases related? Once that is under control, the rest of the proof becomes a special-functions argument.

7. The shift equation: the recursive engine of the proof

The paper introduces

\[ U(\gamma,p) := \mathbb E\!\left[\left(\int_0^{2\pi} e^{\frac{\gamma}{2}X(e^{i\theta})}\,d\theta\right)^p\right], \qquad U(\gamma,p)=(2\pi)^p\mathbb E[Y_\gamma^p]. \]

By analyzing the hypergeometric expansions near the boundary and comparing coefficients, it derives the shift relation

\[ U(\gamma,p) = \frac{2\pi\,\Gamma\!\left(1-\frac{p\gamma^2}{4}\right)} {\Gamma\!\left(1-\frac{\gamma^2}{4}\right)\, \Gamma\!\left(1-\frac{(p-1)\gamma^2}{4}\right)} \,U(\gamma,p-1), \qquad p\le 0. \]

This is the recursive engine of the proof. Starting from \(U(\gamma,0)=1\), the paper obtains all non-positive integer moments. Then it uses moment determinacy and Fourier inversion to identify the law of \(Y_\gamma^{-1}\), transforms back to \(Y_\gamma\), and from that recovers the full moment formula for all real \(p<4/\gamma^2\).

So the proof has a beautiful architecture: a CFT correlation becomes a BPZ equation, the BPZ equation becomes a hypergeometric ODE, the hypergeometric ODE yields a shift relation, and the shift relation pins down the law.

8. Why the answer is so elegant

A natural question is why one should ever expect such a clean Gamma-function answer. The paper’s structure itself gives the clue. Gamma functions are the native language of Mellin transforms, recursive relations, and hypergeometric connection coefficients. Once the problem is converted into a hypergeometric one, Gamma ratios are almost unavoidable. The real achievement is not merely computing the coefficients, but showing that the probabilistic observable lands in exactly the right rigid class of special functions.

\[ \mathbb E[Y_\gamma^{-n}] = \Gamma\!\left(1+\frac{n\gamma^2}{4}\right)\, \Gamma\!\left(1-\frac{\gamma^2}{4}\right)^n, \qquad n\in\mathbb N, \]

and from there the density can be identified explicitly. This is the point where the theorem stops feeling like a collection of formal identities and starts feeling like a complete probabilistic classification.

9. The “beyond” part: what could go wrong analytically?

The later chapters are not an appendix in spirit; they are the conceptual payoff. The paper asks what happens if one perturbs the regularization or the insertion pattern. In particular, it tweaks the regularization by replacing the special boundary weight \(\gamma/2\) inside the exponential insertion by a more general parameter \(\eta\).

The message is subtle. Convergence of the regularized object may still survive under such changes, so one does not immediately lose the random observable. But the exact analytic cancellations that made the BPZ derivation work are no longer guaranteed. The first-derivative formulas change, metric-dependent terms need not vanish in the right way, and the special second-order BPZ structure can fail.

This is an important lesson: the theorem is not just a consequence of “Gaussianity plus effort.” It depends on a very finely tuned choice of insertions and normalizations. The special parameter \(V_{-\gamma/2}\) is not a cosmetic choice; it is the one that brings the degenerate structure needed for a second-order equation. Change the setup slightly, and the rigid ODE machinery can break.

The paper also explores more insertions and a geometric change-of-variable viewpoint. The upshot is that one can still transport the equation and still see hypergeometric structure in the right variables, but the simplicity of the original argument is highly unstable. The beautiful final formula sits at a very special intersection of probability, geometry, and representation theory.

10. The deeper reason: the algebraic viewpoint

The final chapter explains that the BPZ equation is not an accidental differential identity discovered by brave differentiation. It is the analytic shadow of a representation-theoretic fact. The paper introduces the Witt algebra and its central extension, the Virasoro algebra, whose generators satisfy

\[ [L_n,L_m]=(n-m)L_{n+m} +\delta_{n+m,0}\frac{n}{12}(n^2-1)Z. \]

It then discusses Verma modules, Hermitian forms, and the quotienting phenomenon that produces irreducible Virasoro modules. The decisive point is that certain fields correspond to highest-weight representations containing null vectors. Those null vectors are algebraic relations among descendants.

Once translated into CFT language through Ward identities, those algebraic relations become linear differential equations for correlation functions. That is the true origin of the BPZ equation. The computations in the earlier chapter were the analytic realization of an underlying representation-theoretic mechanism.

This perspective also clarifies the “what goes wrong” chapter. If the insertion is no longer in the right degenerate representation, then there is no reason for a second-order BPZ equation to exist. The failure is not just technical; it reflects the absence of the algebraic structure that previously forced the ODE.

11. How to read the whole paper in one sentence

The paper proves an explicit Gamma-function formula for the moments of a boundary GMC partition function by embedding the problem into Liouville CFT, deriving a BPZ equation from a degenerate insertion, converting it into a hypergeometric equation, extracting a shift relation, and then explaining that the whole mechanism works only because of a very rigid algebraic structure in the background.

12. Final takeaway

This is a very nice project because it does two jobs at once. First, it gives a readable path to a famous exact formula in log-correlated probability. Second, it refuses to stop at the formula and instead asks why the proof is so structured. The answer is that the closed moment law is born from a special compatibility: the Gaussian free field provides the log-correlated randomness, GMC turns exponentials of that field into genuine random measures, Liouville CFT packages the observable into correlation functions, the degenerate insertion creates the BPZ equation, hypergeometric theory solves the resulting ODE, and Virasoro representation theory explains why such an ODE should exist at all.

Impact: the paper shows how an exact law in random geometry emerges from a precise chain linking GFF regularization, GMC renormalization, BPZ differential equations, hypergeometric analysis, and Virasoro algebra.
