Fractagon Convergence Theorem

We consider the following geometric probability problem: sample n points from the closed unit disk, order them by polar angle, and connect consecutive points. The resulting angularly induced polygon, which we call the fractagon, has a random signed area An. The central point of the paper is that this area is not asymptotically erratic. On the contrary, it converges almost surely to a deterministic quantity.

The mechanism is already visible in the local geometry. Once the sample is ordered by angle, each edge contributes an oriented triangular term, and the total area is obtained by summing those contributions around the circle. This decomposition is the conceptual center of both the uniform theorem and its generalized version.

\[ A_n = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^{n} R(i)R(i+1)\sin(\Delta\Theta_i), \qquad \sum_{i=1}^{n}\Delta\Theta_i = 2\pi. \]

1. Fractagon Convergence Theorem, Uniform Case

In the first theorem, the sample points are independent and uniformly distributed on the unit disk. The remarkable fact is that the signed area of the corresponding fractagon converges almost surely to a constant. Thus a random polygon, formed from a highly irregular finite sample, acquires a deterministic asymptotic area.

\[ A_n \xrightarrow{\mathrm{a.s.}} \frac{4\pi}{9} \approx 1.3963. \]

The limit is driven by two simple features. First, the average radial contribution is fixed by the uniform law on the disk. Second, the angular spacings become uniformly small, so the polygon resolves the full circle more and more faithfully as the number of vertices increases.

1.1 Probability Space and Sampling Process

Write each sampled point in polar coordinates as \((R_k,\Phi_k)\). For the uniform distribution on the unit disk, the angular variable is uniform on \([0,2\pi)\), the radial variable has distribution function \(F_R(r)=r^2\) and density \(f_R(r)=2r\), and the radial and angular components are independent. The first moment of the radius is therefore \(\mathbb{E}[R]=2/3\), which will appear in the limiting constant.

\[ f_{R,\Phi}(r,\theta)=2r\cdot \frac{1}{2\pi}, \qquad \mathbb{E}[R]=\frac{2}{3}, \qquad \mathbb{E}[R^2]=\frac{1}{2}. \]

1.2 Polygon Construction

Let \(\Phi(1)\leq \cdots \leq \Phi(n)\) be the angular order statistics, and let \(R(i)\) denote the radius paired with \(\Phi(i)\). The fractagon is the polygon obtained by joining these vertices cyclically. Its geometry is controlled by the angular spacings \(\Delta\Theta_i\), which form a random partition of the circle.

\[ \Delta\Theta_i= \begin{cases} \Phi(i+1)-\Phi(i), & 1\leq i\leq n-1,\\[4pt] 2\pi-\Phi(n)+\Phi(1), & i=n. \end{cases} \]

The crucial point is that, once the angles are sorted, the area is still expressed by the same local wedge formula. Each consecutive pair contributes an oriented triangular term, and the global object is a sum of these local pieces.

1.3 Proof of the Main Result

The proof proceeds in three steps. First, one computes the conditional expectation of the area given the angles. Second, one shows that the sine sum associated with the angular spacings converges to \(2\pi\). Third, one proves that the centered fluctuation is small enough, in fourth moment, to vanish almost surely.

\[ \mathbb{E}[A_n \mid G_n] = \frac{2}{9}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\sin(\Delta\Theta_i). \]

Then, there comes an application of what I like to call "Extremal Gap Theory", in particular, that on the unit interval: Since the maximal spacing tends to zero almost surely, the approximation \(\sin x \sim x\) becomes effective over the entire partition. Hence the random trigonometric sum recovers the full angular mass of the circle.

\[ \sum_{i=1}^{n}\sin(\Delta\Theta_i)\xrightarrow{\mathrm{a.s.}} 2\pi. \]

What remains is to control the difference between the area and its conditional expectation. This is done through a fourth-moment estimate and a Borel–Cantelli argument.

\[ \mathbb{E}\!\left[\left|A_n-\mathbb{E}[A_n\mid G_n]\right|^4\right] = O\!\left(\frac{1}{n^2}\right). \]

Combining the conditional limit with the concentration estimate yields the theorem. The asymptotic area is therefore the product of a radial mean-square mechanism and an angular partition mechanism.

2. Fractagon Convergence Theorem, Version 2

The second theorem removes the independence between radius and angle. The angular marginal remains uniform, but the radial component may now depend on direction. In that setting, the limiting area is governed by the regression function \(m(\theta)=\mathbb{E}[R\mid \Phi=\theta]\).

\[ A_n \xrightarrow{\mathrm{a.s.}} \frac{1}{2}\int_{0}^{2\pi} m(\theta)^2\,d\theta = \frac{1}{2}\|m\|_{L^2([0,2\pi))}^2. \]

This formula shows that the limit is no longer determined by a constant radial mean. Instead, it records the full directional profile of the sample through the square of the conditional expectation. The independent case is recovered when \(m\) is constant.

2.1 Conditional Independence Structure

After conditioning on the angles, the radii remain independent, but their laws now depend on the observed directions. The concomitant \(R(i)\) has the conditional law of the radius given \(\Phi=\Phi(i)\), and this allows the same wedge decomposition to be retained with angle-dependent coefficients.

\[ \mathbb{E}[R(i)\mid G_n]=m(\Phi(i)), \qquad \mathbb{E}[R(i)R(j)\mid G_n] = m(\Phi(i))\,m(\Phi(j)), \quad i\neq j. \]

2.2 Convergence of the Conditional Expectation

The conditional area becomes a random Riemann sum over the angular partition. The Lipschitz regularity of \(m\) makes it possible to replace \(\sin(\Delta\Theta_i)\) by \(\Delta\Theta_i\), then replace the adjacent product \(m(\Phi(i))m(\Phi(i+1))\) by the square \(m(\Phi(i))^2\), and finally pass from the discrete sum to the integral of \(m^2\).

\[ \mathbb{E}[A_n\mid G_n] = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^{n} m(\Phi(i))m(\Phi(i+1))\sin(\Delta\Theta_i) \xrightarrow{\mathrm{a.s.}} \frac{1}{2}\int_{0}^{2\pi} m(\theta)^2\,d\theta. \]

The error terms are controlled by \(\sum_i (\Delta\Theta_i)^2\), which vanishes almost surely because the maximal spacing tends to zero. In this way, the angular partition becomes asymptotically fine enough to recover the continuous quadratic profile of \(m\).

2.3 Almost Sure Convergence

The final argument has the same structure as in the uniform case. After subtracting the conditional expectation, one obtains a centered, conditionally 1-dependent sequence. The same fourth-moment method gives summable tail bounds, and Borel–Cantelli completes the proof.

\[ A_n - \mathbb{E}[A_n\mid G_n] \xrightarrow{\mathrm{a.s.}} 0. \]

The figures in the paper are therefore not only illustrative, but are in fact the proof in geometric form. The key idea is indeed to measure the area of each of the triangles and then add them up. Each edge contributes a local signed area term of the form \(\frac{1}{2}R(i)R(i+1)\sin(\Delta\Theta_i)\), and the theorem shows that these local contributions assemble into a deterministic global limit. In the uniform case that limit is \(4\pi/9\); in the general case it is the quadratic quantity \(\frac{1}{2}\int_0^{2\pi} m(\theta)^2\,d\theta\).

Impact: the fractagon provides a clean example in which random discrete geometry converges to a deterministic limit through the interaction of angular spacings, conditional expectation, and almost sure concentration.
