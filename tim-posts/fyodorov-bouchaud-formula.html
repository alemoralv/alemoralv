FB Formula and BPZ Equations

This research project studies an object which at first seems too singular to admit an exact law: the partition function of Gaussian multiplicative chaos on the unit circle. The surprise is that, once the problem is placed in the language of Liouville conformal field theory, the randomness becomes rigid enough to satisfy differential equations, hypergeometric identities, and eventually an explicit distributional formula.

The project is not only about proving one famous identity. It also clarifies why the relevant BPZ equation appears, proposes a generalized BPZ framework, studies semiclassical and rare-event consequences, and then reinterprets the same structure dynamically through SLEs and algebraically through the Virasoro algebra. In that sense, the formula is the center of the story, but not the whole story.

Foundations

The starting point is the Gaussian free field, not as an honest function, but as a random distribution with covariance given by a Green kernel. This already explains why the problem is delicate: in two dimensions the covariance has logarithmic singularities, so one cannot simply evaluate the field pointwise and exponentiate it.

\[ \Delta G(x,\cdot)=\delta_x(\cdot), \]

together with appropriate boundary conditions, is the equation underlying the covariance kernel. The field is then interpreted distributionally, and one regularizes it by smooth approximations \(X_\varepsilon\). This lets one define the Gaussian multiplicative chaos through a renormalized exponential.

\[ M_\varepsilon(dz) = e^{\gamma X_\varepsilon(z)-\frac{\gamma^2}{2}\mathbb{E}[X_\varepsilon(z)^2]}\,\sigma(dz), \qquad \gamma\in(0,2). \]

The limit of these measures, as \(\varepsilon\downarrow 0\), is the GMC measure. This is the random geometry behind the whole project. The logarithmic divergence is not removed by magic, but by the exact renormalization above. What remains is a nontrivial random measure with a very rich moment structure.

We then shift toward Liouville conformal field theory. This is an essential move: instead of studying a single random integral in isolation, the project embeds it into a family of correlation functions built from vertex operators.

\[ V_\alpha(z) = e^{\alpha\left(X(z)+\frac{Q}{2}\log \hat g(z)\right)}, \qquad \Delta_\alpha=\frac{\alpha}{2}\left(Q-\frac{\alpha}{2}\right), \qquad Q=\frac{2}{\gamma}+\frac{\gamma}{2}. \]

So, this first part builds the dictionary: rough field \(\to\) regularization \(\to\) chaos measure \(\to\) Liouville correlation functions. Without that dictionary, the main formula would look isolated. With it, the formula becomes one manifestation of a larger conformal structure.

Fyodorov–Bouchaud Formula: Statement and Proof

The central random variable of the project is the partition function

\[ Y_\gamma = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} e^{\frac{\gamma}{2}X(e^{i\theta})}\,d\theta, \qquad \gamma\in(0,2). \]

The main theorem states that for every \(p<4/\gamma^2\),

\[ \mathbb{E}[Y_\gamma^p] = \frac{\Gamma\!\left(1-\frac{p\gamma^2}{4}\right)} {\Gamma\!\left(1-\frac{\gamma^2}{4}\right)^p}. \]

Equivalently,

\[ Y_\gamma \stackrel{d}{=} \frac{1}{\Gamma\!\left(1-\frac{\gamma^2}{4}\right)\,E(1)^{\gamma^2/4}}, \]

where \(E(1)\) is an exponential random variable of parameter \(1\). This is the Fyodorov–Bouchaud formula. The final expression is classical in appearance, but the object it describes is genuinely singular.

The project proves this result through LCFT, and here one of its main novelties appears: instead of only using the classical degenerate insertion, it introduces a tweaked regularization with an extra parameter \(\eta\) and asks for which values a BPZ equation still survives. This makes precise why the usual choice is special.

\[ \left( \frac{1}{\alpha_1^2}\partial_{z_1z_1} + \frac{\Delta_{\alpha_1}}{(z_1-\bar z_1)^2} + \frac{\Delta_{\alpha_2}}{(z_1-z_2)^2} + \frac{\Delta_{\alpha_2}}{(z_1-\bar z_2)^2} + \frac{1}{z_1-\bar z_1}\partial_{\bar z_1} + \frac{1}{z_1-z_2}\partial_{z_2} + \frac{1}{z_1-\bar z_2}\partial_{\bar z_2} \right) \langle V_{\alpha_1}(z_1)V_{\alpha_2}(z_2)\rangle_{H,\eta} =0. \]

The project shows that this holds precisely for the distinguished choices \[ (\alpha_1,\eta)=\left(-\frac{\gamma}{2},\frac{\gamma}{2}\right) \quad\text{or}\quad (\alpha_1,\eta)=\left(-\frac{2}{\gamma},\frac{2}{\gamma}\right). \] So the usual degenerate insertion is not merely convenient: it is singled out by an exact compatibility condition.

The proof mechanism is elegant and concrete. Girsanov’s theorem is used repeatedly to shift the Gaussian field, derive integration-by-parts identities, and compute derivatives of the correlation function. Metric-dependent terms initially appear, but they cancel out thanks to a structural identity. This is one of the most important parts of the project: geometry seems to complicate the formulas, and then the geometry itself removes the complication.

After that, the problem is pushed from the upper half-plane to the unit disk by conformal maps. A Möbius transform then compresses the geometry of two marked points into a single parameter

\[ t = \left|\frac{z_1-z_2}{z_1-\bar z_2}\right|, \qquad t\in(0,1). \]

This is the geometric step that turns the BPZ PDE into a hypergeometric ODE. Once that happens, the negative moments of the partition function can be encoded in a hypergeometric expansion. The asymptotics at \(t=0\) and \(t=1\) produce shift relations, and those relations ultimately reconstruct the entire moment formula.

Another novelty is that the generalized BPZ mechanism is not restricted to two-point functions. The project extends it to \(n\)-point correlation functions. This part then clarifies the structural reason behind the proof and pushes it into a broader framework.

Fyodorov–Bouchaud Formula: Consequences

Once the exact law is known, the project turns to asymptotic consequences. The first one is the semiclassical limit, where the coupling becomes small and the random object starts to exhibit a more classical behavior. This is where exact formulas become especially powerful: they can be expanded, rescaled, and interpreted.

The chapter also studies rare events through large deviations. This is a natural question in Gaussian multiplicative chaos, since the measure is designed precisely to capture highly uneven concentrations of mass. The rate function then describes how unlikely certain rescaled values become as the coupling tends to zero.

In words, this part asks: once the partition function is explicit, what does it tell us about typical behavior, atypical behavior, and the transition between the two? That is why this chapter is more than an appendix to the theorem. It shows that the exact law has real asymptotic content.

BPZ Equations: A Dynamical and Algebraic Viewpoint

The last chapter of the project gives it its broadest perspective. The same BPZ equations that controlled the GMC partition function are now re-read dynamically through Schramm–Loewner evolutions and algebraically through the Virasoro algebra. This is one of the most appealing aspects of the project: the same differential equation belongs at once to probability, complex dynamics, and representation theory.

4.1 BPZ Equations and SLEs

The dynamical viewpoint begins with the chordal Loewner equation on the upper half-plane:

\[ \begin{cases} \displaystyle \frac{\partial}{\partial t}g_t(z)=\frac{2}{g_t(z)-\sqrt{\kappa}\,B_t},\\[1ex] g_0(z)=z. \end{cases} \]

This is the SLE flow. One should think of \(g_t\) as a random conformal evolution driven by Brownian motion. The project then broadens the picture by allowing a semimartingale driver

\[ dX_t=\kappa\,b(X_t)\,dt+\sqrt{\kappa}\,dB_t, \qquad X_0=0. \]

Via Girsanov, this can be related back to the Brownian-driven case, so the generalized setting is not disconnected from the classical one. It is a controlled deformation of it.

A key idea in the chapter is that the conformal images of marked points, together with the derivatives of the Loewner map, naturally generate martingales whenever a certain differential equation holds. Differentiating the Loewner equation in \(z\) gives

\[ \partial_t g_t'(z) = -\frac{2\,g_t'(z)}{(g_t(z)-X_t)^2}, \qquad dg_t'(z) = -\frac{2\,g_t'(z)}{(g_t(z)-X_t)^2}\,dt. \]

From this the project introduces

\[ G_t:=\prod_{i=1}^n g_t'(z_i)^{\Delta_{\alpha_i}}, \]

and derives the evolution

\[ dG_t = -2G_t\sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\Delta_{\alpha_i}}{(g_t(z_i)-X_t)^2}\,dt. \]

This is the point where the BPZ structure enters dynamically. If one writes

\[ M_t(z_1,\dots,z_n) = G_t\,h(g_t(z_1),\dots,g_t(z_n)), \]

then the project shows that \(M_t\) is a local martingale if and only if \(h\) satisfies a BPZ-type PDE. In the SLE setting recorded in the project, the condition is

\[ \left( \frac{\kappa}{2} \sum_{i,j=1}^n \partial_x\Phi(t,x,g_t^{-1}(y_i)) \partial_x\Phi(t,x,g_t^{-1}(y_j)) \frac{\partial^2}{\partial y_i\partial y_j} + \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{2}{y_i-x}\frac{\partial}{\partial y_i} - \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{2\Delta_{\alpha_i}}{(y_i-x)^2} \right) h(y_1,\dots,y_n)=0. \]

So the BPZ equation is no longer just a static conformal-field-theoretic identity. It becomes exactly the condition that makes a naturally constructed SLE observable into a martingale. This is a very clean dynamical meaning.

The project goes further and records the stochastic differential of the martingale itself:

\[ dM_t = \sqrt{\kappa}\,G_t \sum_{i=1}^n \partial_x\Phi(t,X_t,z_i)\, (\partial_i h)(g_t(z_1),\dots,g_t(z_n))\,dB_t. \]

When this local martingale is a true martingale, one gets a mean-value representation:

\[ h(z_1,\dots,z_n) = \mathbb{E}\!\left[ h(g_t(z_1),\dots,g_t(z_n)) \prod_{i=1}^n \bigl((\partial_z\Phi)(t,X_t,z_i)\bigr)^{\Delta_{\alpha_i}} \right]. \]

This is a particularly nice formula. It says that the correlation-type quantity \(h\) can be recovered as an average of its image under the random conformal flow, weighted by the conformal derivatives. In other words, BPZ observables are transported by the SLE dynamics in a martingale-preserving way.

The chapter then reaches one of its most beautiful interpretations: certain three-point correlation functions are actual probabilities. More precisely, if one considers an SLE from \(0\) to \(\infty\), the probability that the curve passes to the left of a marked point \(z_1\in\mathbb H\) is identified with a Liouville three-point function:

\[ \mathbb{P}\!\left(\text{SLE from }0\text{ to }\infty\text{ passes to the left of }z_1\right) = \bigl\langle V_{-\gamma/2}(z_1)V_{\alpha_2}(0)V_{\alpha_3}(\infty)\bigr\rangle_{H,\gamma/2}. \]

This is the point where the project’s dynamical viewpoint becomes especially vivid. A correlation function is no longer merely a formal object in a field theory; it is the probability of a geometric event involving a random curve. That is a remarkable bridge between LCFT and stochastic planar geometry.

The project even suggests a possible discrete analogue: a meromorphic random dynamical system with noisy iteration, meant as a discrete counterpart to the continuous Loewner flow. This gives the chapter a forward-looking quality. It does not only reinterpret known equations; it hints at further directions in random complex dynamics.

4.2 BPZ Equations and Virasoro Algebras

The final section explains why BPZ equations should exist from an algebraic point of view. The Witt algebra is generated by vector fields

\[ L_n := z^{1-n}\frac{d}{dz}, \qquad [L_n,L_m]=(n-m)L_{n+m}, \qquad n,m\in\mathbb Z. \]

Its central extension is the Virasoro algebra. In the background of BPZ theory lies the fact that, for special conformal weights, the corresponding representations become degenerate. Those degeneracies force the conformal blocks to satisfy linear differential equations. The BPZ equations are thus not accidental analytic facts; they are shadows of representation-theoretic constraints.

This closes the circle of the project. A random partition function from Gaussian multiplicative chaos leads to LCFT correlation functions; those satisfy BPZ equations; the same BPZ equations appear as martingale conditions in SLE; and their existence is explained algebraically by Virasoro degeneracy. The formula sits at the meeting point of all these viewpoints.

Impact: this research project shows that the partition function of Gaussian multiplicative chaos on the circle is not merely computable, but structurally rigid. Its exact law is uncovered through LCFT, its special differential equations are clarified through a generalized BPZ framework, its asymptotics reveal semiclassical and rare-event behavior, and its dynamical meaning emerges through SLE martingales and geometric probabilities.
