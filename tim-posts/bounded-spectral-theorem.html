BF-Calculus and Spectral Theorem

This bachelor thesis studies how one can pass from the familiar finite-dimensional spectral theorem to a fully infinite-dimensional representation theorem for bounded self-adjoint operators on separable Hilbert spaces. The central problem is not merely to diagonalize an operator in an abstract sense, but to construct a precise rule that assigns an operator f(M) to every bounded Borel-measurable function f on the spectrum of M.

The final target is a spectral representation in which the operator becomes multiplication by the scalar function itself. In this sense, the whole thesis may be read as a carefully organized passage from algebraic expressions in powers of M to measurable functional expressions over its spectrum. The main point is that self-adjoint operators are not only objects that admit eigenvalue-like decompositions in finite dimensions, but also operators that can be understood through measure-theoretic and topological machinery in the infinite-dimensional setting.

\[ f(M)=\int_{\sigma(M)} f\,dP=\mathcal{U}\mathcal{M}_f\mathcal{U}^* \]

Here, \(\mathcal{M}_f\) denotes multiplication by \(f\) on a suitable Hilbert space of sections, \(P\) is the spectral measure associated with \(M\), and \(\mathcal{U}\) is an isometric isomorphism transporting multiplication on the spectral side back to the original Hilbert space.

\[ \begin{array}{ccc} L^2(\sigma(M);\chi) & \xrightarrow{\ \mathcal{M}_f\ } & L^2(\sigma(M);\chi) \\ \mathcal{U}\downarrow & & \downarrow\mathcal{U} \\ H & \xrightarrow{\ f(M)\ } & H \end{array} \]

Chapter 1 — Finite-Dimensional Spectral Theorem.

The thesis begins in the finite-dimensional setting, where the spectral theorem is already known from linear algebra. If \(M=M^*\in\mathcal L(H)\) and \(H\cong \ell^2(\{1,\dots,n\})\), then there exists a unitary operator \(U\) and a diagonal operator \(\Lambda=\mathrm{diag}(\lambda_1,\dots,\lambda_n)\) such that \[ M=U\Lambda U^*. \] This is the point where the geometric structure first becomes visible: the operator is decomposed into spectral blocks, and each eigenvalue \(\lambda\) comes with an orthogonal projection onto its eigenspace.

Instead of viewing this merely as diagonalization, the thesis rewrites the operator as a sum over spectral projections:

\[ M=\sum_{\lambda\in\sigma(M)} \lambda P_\lambda. \]

This already suggests a functional calculus. Indeed, if one can define \(f(M)\) by acting on each spectral block with the scalar \(f(\lambda)\), then one is naturally led to

\[ f(M)=\sum_{\lambda\in\sigma(M)} f(\lambda)P_\lambda. \]

The crucial insight is that the spectral theorem is not only a statement about one operator \(M\), but also about an entire algebra of operators generated from \(M\) by functions. This finite-dimensional model serves as the conceptual prototype for the rest of the thesis: later, sums will become integrals, eigenspaces will become measurable fibers, and diagonal multiplication will survive as the correct final form.

Chapter 2 — Overture.

The second chapter prepares the technical framework. At first sight, it may seem far removed from spectral theory: it introduces initial topologies, weak and weak* topologies, nets, dense extension principles, and measurable Hilbert bundles. Yet this chapter is essential because the later extension procedures are not purely algebraic; they depend on choosing the right topologies and the right notion of continuity.

One of the conceptual pillars is the characterization of convergence in an initial topology. If \(\mathcal F\subseteq Y^X\) is a family of functions and \(X\) is endowed with the weakest topology making all \(f\in\mathcal F\) continuous, then a net \((x_a)\) converges to \(x\) precisely when all observable coordinates converge:

\[ x_a\to x \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \forall f\in\mathcal F:\ f(x_a)\to f(x). \]

This perspective becomes decisive later, when Borel functions and operators are equipped with topologies weaker than the norm topology. Another major ingredient is the construction of measurable Hilbert bundles \(\chi\), together with the associated spaces \(L^2(\Omega;\chi)\). These are the natural infinite-dimensional replacements for the direct sum of eigenspaces from Chapter 1. In finite dimensions one decomposes the Hilbert space into spectral pieces; here one allows the fiber over each point of the spectrum to be a Hilbert space, and one studies square-integrable sections over the whole spectral set.

Thus, Chapter 2 does not yet prove the spectral theorem, but it builds the language in which the final theorem can even be formulated.

Chapter 3 — Continuous Functional Calculus.

The third chapter carries out the first substantial extension step. It starts from polynomials. Given \(p(x)=\sum_{n=0}^N a_nx^n\), one defines

\[ p(M):=\sum_{n=0}^N a_n M^n. \]

This is the most natural possible definition, but the important point is not its formal appearance; it is the structural statement that follows. The polynomial map \(p\mapsto p(M)\) is shown to be a unital, isometric algebra \(*\)-homomorphism. In particular, multiplication, adjoints, and norms are preserved in exactly the way one hopes for. This means the operator calculus respects the algebraic and analytic structure of the function space.

The chapter then proves the spectrum-mapping principle in the polynomial case and extends the entire construction to continuous functions on the compact spectrum \(\sigma(M)\). The key mechanism is Stone–Weierstrass approximation: continuous functions can be approximated uniformly by polynomials, and the operator-side construction is rigid enough to pass to the limit.

\[ \sigma\bigl(f(M)\bigr)=f\bigl(\sigma(M)\bigr), \qquad f\in \mathcal C(\sigma(M)). \]

This chapter is where the thesis first makes clear that the operator \(M\) is being converted into a genuine functional object: one is no longer restricted to algebraic expressions in \(M\), but may apply arbitrary continuous functions to it.

Chapter 4 — Advanced Tools from Topology and Functional Analysis.

The fourth chapter supplies the heavy functional-analytic machinery needed for the next extension step. The transition from continuous functions to bounded Borel-measurable functions is subtler than the transition from polynomials to continuous functions. Uniform approximation is no longer sufficient, and one needs duality, weak* compactness, and operator topologies.

For this reason, the thesis develops Goldstine's theorem, studies weaker notions of convergence, and proves that \(\mathcal L(H)\) is a dual space via trace class operators. Conceptually, the point is that bounded operators must be placed in a setting where weak* arguments become available. Once \(\mathcal L(H)\) is recognized as a dual object, one can exploit bidual extensions without losing control of the algebraic structure established earlier.

This chapter therefore acts as the analytical bridge between the continuous world and the measurable world. It is not ornamental scaffolding: without it, the Borel extension diagram of the next chapter would have no foundation.

Chapter 5 — Borel Functional Calculus.

This is the conceptual center of the thesis. The task is now to extend the continuous functional calculus to all bounded Borel-measurable functions on the spectrum. The guiding idea is that one should not define the extension ad hoc, but rather construct it through a commutative diagram involving biduals and canonical embeddings.

\[ \begin{array}{ccccc} \mathbb K[x]\!\mid_{\sigma(M)} & \subset & \mathcal C(\sigma(M)) & \subset & \mathcal B(\sigma(M)) \\ & & \downarrow \Gamma & & \downarrow \widehat{\Gamma} \\ & & \mathcal L(H) & \subset & \mathcal L(H)'' \end{array} \]

The philosophy is simple but powerful: first understand the continuous calculus as an operator between Banach spaces, then pass to the bidual, and finally return to \(\mathcal L(H)\) through the canonical projection. In this way, one obtains an extension \(f\mapsto f(M)\) for every bounded Borel-measurable \(f\), while preserving the decisive properties of the earlier stages.

The chapter then turns to indicator functions. These are crucial because they generate measurable structure. Once one sets

\[ P(S):= 1_S(M), \]

the operator calculus immediately begins to behave like a measure. Indeed, the family \(P\) becomes a spectral measure, and this is the moment when the thesis shifts from function calculus to operator-valued integration. In finite dimensions, projections onto eigenspaces were the atoms of the theory. Here, indicator functions produce the measurable analogue of those atoms.

Chapter 6 — Integration with Spectral Measures.

Once the spectral measure is available, the next natural step is to integrate with respect to it. The sixth chapter constructs the spectral integral by following the same hierarchy that underlies the ordinary Lebesgue integral: first indicator functions, then simple functions, and finally bounded Borel-measurable functions.

The starting prescription is

\[ \int_\Omega 1_S\,dP := 1_S(M). \]

From there, linearity extends the definition to simple functions, and a uniform approximation argument extends it further to bounded measurable functions. What matters is that the resulting map remains a unital algebra \(*\)-homomorphism and agrees with the Borel functional calculus already constructed in Chapter 5.

\[ \int_\Omega f\,dP \quad\text{is defined so that}\quad f(M)=\int_{\sigma(M)} f\,dP. \]

This identity is more than a notational convenience. It says that the abstract extension procedure of Chapter 5 and the concrete integration procedure of Chapter 6 produce the same operator. Thus the two viewpoints—functional calculus and spectral integration—are proven to be one and the same theory.

Chapter 7 — Bounded Spectral Theorem.

The final chapter gathers every thread of the thesis into a single representation theorem. Starting from a bounded self-adjoint operator \(M\), one first has a Borel functional calculus, then a spectral measure, then a spectral integral. The last step is to show that the whole theory can be represented on a Hilbert space of sections where \(M\) becomes multiplication by the identity function and \(f(M)\) becomes multiplication by \(f\).

The final theorem produces three objects: a probability measure \(\mu\) on the spectrum, a measurable Hilbert bundle \(\chi\) over \(\sigma(M)\), and an isometric isomorphism \[ \mathcal U:L^2(\sigma(M);\chi)\to H. \] With these in hand, one obtains the commuting picture announced at the start:

\[ \begin{array}{ccc} L^2(\sigma(M);\chi) & \xrightarrow{\ \mathcal M_f\ } & L^2(\sigma(M);\chi) \\ \mathcal U\downarrow & & \downarrow\mathcal U \\ H & \xrightarrow{\ f(M)\ } & H \end{array} \]

In particular, taking \(f=\mathrm{id}\) yields the spectral resolution of the operator itself:

\[ M=\mathcal U\mathcal M_{\mathrm{id}}\mathcal U^*. \]

At this stage the analogy with finite-dimensional diagonalization is complete, but the thesis makes clear that the infinite-dimensional result is not obtained by analogy alone. It is built through a chain of carefully justified extensions: polynomial, continuous, Borel, measure-valued, integral, and finally representation-theoretic.

Logical architecture of the thesis.

\[ \text{Finite-dimensional model} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{Polynomial FC} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{Continuous FC} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{Borel FC} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{Spectral Measure} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{Spectral Integral} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{Bounded Spectral Theorem.} \]

This is what gives the thesis its coherence. Each chapter contributes a necessary layer, and the final theorem does not arrive as a sudden formal statement but as the inevitable end point of the preceding structure. The reader is led from a concrete linear-algebraic intuition to an operator-theoretic theorem whose natural home is measure theory and Hilbert space geometry.

Impact: the thesis gives a conceptually transparent route from diagonalization in finite dimensions to the full bounded spectral theorem, showing how topology, duality, measurable structure, and operator algebras cooperate to turn a self-adjoint operator into multiplication on a spectral space.
